Origin & Motivation

The Thread of Abstraction

It began with a single theorem I could not prove. In elementary number theory, Hall's theorem on solvable groups seemed impenetrable — until a footnote pointed to Representation Theory as the key. That footnote changed everything.

A summer reading project on Fulton & Harris later, the door swung open. Burnside's theorem fell. Hall's theorem fell. And with them, a conviction formed: the most powerful mathematics is rarely confined to a single domain. The abstractions talk to each other.

Then came Algebraic Number Theory, and a second revelation. Theorems I had accepted as clever tricks — quadratic reciprocity chief among them — emerged as natural consequences of a profound underlying structure. The Artin map. Dedekind–Kummer. Kronecker–Weber. A whole edifice, each piece supporting the next.

I now pursue Class Field Theory, Algebraic Geometry, and their intersections, driven by a simple belief: there is always a more fundamental reason why something is true.

Scholarly Work

Reading Projects

April – May 2025

Representation Theory of Finite Groups

Prof. Maneesh Thakur  ·  ISI Bengaluru

This project began as a search for a proof of Hall's theorem — a result that resisted every classical technique I knew. The answer lay in representation theory. Working through Fulton & Harris, I covered characters and their applications, induced representations, Frobenius reciprocity, and the combinatorial beauty of Young diagrams. The project culminated in proving Burnside's theorem and, at last, Hall's theorem on solvable groups. The experience crystallised a guiding principle: abstract machinery often illuminates what direct methods obscure.

Fulton & Harris — Representation Theory Young Tableaux & Symmetriser Frobenius Reciprocity

June – July 2025

Algebraic Number Theory

Prof. Prem Prakash Pandey  ·  IISER Berhampur

A turning point. Theorems I had accepted as clever tricks — quadratic reciprocity above all — revealed themselves as consequences of a deeper grammar. The Artin map and Dedekind–Kummer theorem made reciprocity entirely natural: a classification of how primes split, not a computation to be memorised. By the project's end, Prof. Pandey had introduced us to the Kronecker–Weber theorem, Hilbert's 12th problem, and a tantalising connection to the Brumer–Stark conjecture. The horizon of Class Field Theory opened.

Murty & Esmonde — Problems in ANT Janusz — Algebraic Number Fields Marcus — Number Fields Ch. 5 Lang's ANT

November – December 2025

Representation Theory of Compact Lie Groups

Prof. Pralay Chatterjee  ·  IMSc Chennai

Harmonic analysis and Lie theory converged here. Working through Sepanski's treatment of compact Lie groups, I encountered Clifford algebras, the Peter–Weyl theorem, and the Plancherel theorem — which generalises Fourier analysis to a far broader setting. Crucially, many definitions from my differential geometry course that had seemed arbitrary acquired their true geometric motivation. The project connected threads from function spaces, representation theory, and geometry in ways that felt inevitable in retrospect.

Sepanski — Compact Lie Groups Peter–Weyl Theorem Clifford Algebras & Spin Groups

Current Focus

Research Interests

Class Field Theory

Pursuing Milne's notes to understand the structural theorem behind the Kronecker–Weber theorem and Artin reciprocity. The Chebotarev density theorem — from which Dirichlet's theorem on primes in progressions falls as a corollary — stands as a landmark on this path.

Algebraic Geometry

Currently immersed in Atiyah–Macdonald and an active course in Algebraic Geometry. Interested in the geometric language that unifies arithmetic — particularly the connections between schemes, Galois theory, and the geometry of number fields.

Analytic Number Theory

Explored through the Madhava Nurture Camp at CMI. L-functions, density theorems, and the mysterious interface between analysis and arithmetic — where the Riemann hypothesis lives — are compelling targets for deeper study.
